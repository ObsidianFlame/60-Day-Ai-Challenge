Build Operation Lifeline: Supply Chain Crisis Lab
Lead an enterprise through a supply chain crisis

What I built:
For Day 29 I built Operation Lifeline: Supply Chain Crisis Lab, a fully offline single-file HTML enterprise simulation that requires zero internet connection to run. 
Players take the role of an operations leader guiding a randomly generated company through an active supply chain crisis across 8 interactive screens: a company profile with 4 data views, a crisis detection alert with urgency and impact scoring, 
a war room where you select 3 of 6 response actions with live metric feedback updating in real time, a 4-round supplier negotiation, a CEO boardroom with 5 executive decision scenarios, 
an AI investment strategy round where you allocate budget across 5 capability options, and a final scored dashboard across 6 weighted dimensions. The simulation draws from a pool of 5 company archetypes and 8 distinct crisis types, fully randomised on every playthrough with no two runs identical.

What I learned:
Supply chain crisis simulation translates well to a general business audience, but healthcare adds a layer of complexity that a generic model undersells. Patient safety, regulatory accountability, and market trust are not simply extra variables — they fundamentally change the stakes of every decision, making the trade-offs harder and the consequences more human.
The exercise also surfaced an important principle about AI-scored judgment. Scoring professional decision-making is genuinely powerful when the purpose is self-improvement and capability development — it accelerates learning, surfaces blind spots, and makes abstract leadership skills tangible. 
But the same mechanism becomes problematic the moment it influences hiring decisions. A simulation, however well designed, is a fringe approximation of real-world judgment, and using it as a deciding factor would systematically disadvantage candidates whose strengths do not map neatly onto branching scenario formats. The tool is valuable. The misuse of the tool is the risk.
