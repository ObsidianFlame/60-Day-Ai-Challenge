Primary Evidence Sources

WHO Healthy Diet Fact Sheet (Updated 2023)

Core guidelines: total fat <30% of energy, saturated fat <10%, trans fat <1%, free sugars <10%, sodium <5g/day (<2g Na), 400g+ fruits & vegetables, 25g+ fiber. NutriScope uses these as the basis for fat, sodium, and fiber risk thresholds. Source →

Harvard T.H. Chan — The Nutrition Source: WHO Updated Fat Guidelines (2023)

Fat quality matters more than fat quantity. Replacing saturated and trans fats with unsaturated fats reduces cardiovascular risk regardless of total fat intake. Whole food plant-based protein sources independently reduce CVD risk. Source →

2026 Global Nutrition Report — Independent Expert Group (Ghosh & Zanello)

2.6 billion people cannot afford a healthy diet. Dietary shifts toward plant-based foods can increase micronutrient deficiency risk without parallel health system action. Iron, B12, zinc, vitamin D are primary watchpoints. Climate change is reducing nutritional quality of staple crops globally.

WHO Guidelines on Physical Activity and Sedentary Behaviour (2020)

150–300 min/week moderate-intensity or 75–150 min vigorous aerobic activity recommended for adults. Activity level drives TDEE and water loss estimates in NutriScope. Source →

USDA FoodData Central & National Institute of Nutrition India (NIN)

Primary sources for all 60 food nutritional values. ICMR-NIN Dietary Guidelines (2024) inform RDA values relevant to South Asian dietary patterns common in the UAE professional population. USDA →

Key Insights
01

WHO 2023: fat quality outweighs fat quantity

Updated guidelines de-emphasise total fat as a risk marker. Replacing saturated and trans fats with unsaturated sources (nuts, avocado, oily fish) reduces CVD risk independent of total fat — a critical distinction for MENA dietary planning where nuts and olive oil are staples.

02

GNR 2026: 2.6 billion cannot afford a healthy diet

The systemic affordability gap underscores why evidence-based dietary tools that translate complex nutrition science into accessible, actionable guidance are a public health priority — not a luxury feature.

03

Sodium is the most underestimated risk factor in UAE diets

WHO recommends <5g salt (<2000mg Na) per day. South Asian and Middle Eastern dietary patterns — relying on processed foods, sauces, bread, and restaurant meals — routinely exceed this by 50–80%. NutriScope treats sodium as a limit metric, never a target.

04

Plant-based transitions need parallel micronutrient strategy

GNR 2026 explicitly cautions that shifts toward plant-based diets can increase deficiency risk in B12, iron, zinc, and omega-3 unless paired with supplementation, fortification, or deliberate food pairing. NutriScope tracks all four for this reason.

05

Vitamin D deficiency is structurally endemic in UAE professionals

The paradox of living in one of the sunniest regions on earth: indoor working hours, year-round air conditioning, and full-body covering eliminate solar UVB synthesis. Vitamin D supplementation is clinically indicated for most UAE residents — diet alone cannot close the gap.
