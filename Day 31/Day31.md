Build an AI Supply Chain Control Tower
Manage live disruptions like the Head of Operations

What i learned:
I built a supply chain decision simulator. You step in as Head of Operations at a fictional logistics company. Disruptions hit one at a time: port congestion with 14 containers stuck, a supplier delay threatening six production lines, a fleet breakdown with three delivery SLAs at risk. 
You have 75 seconds to decide. Hover any option and an Oracle advisory panel reveals the trade-off. Two sides. No recommended answer. Click your choice, see the consequences, then face the next situation. Some decisions trigger cascade events that arrive as the very next brief.
Three difficulty settings. Easy and Medium give you full data and the Oracle. Hard cuts the window to 45 seconds, switches the Oracle off, and hides the impact numbers. Just the situation. Just the options.

What I built:
The first build didn't fail because of one thing. It failed because of everything at once — time pressure, multiple alerts firing simultaneously, a UI that was fighting me, and too much text competing for attention. The moment I knew something was wrong was when I felt my heart rate go up instead of my thinking sharpen. 
That told me something important about training design: you can layer complexity, but the foundation has to be functional first. A tool that distracts is a tool that teaches nothing. 
If I were building a training module for my own team, the UI would be signed off before a single learning objective went near it.
The Oracle panel works because it respects the learner. It gives you both sides of a trade-off and then gets out of the way. You still have to decide. That matters because understanding a supply chain is not about memorising correct answers — it is about seeing how one choice closes certain doors and opens others. 
I apply the same principle when I am advising a team on a decision. My job is to surface the consequences clearly, not to remove the decision from the person who owns it.
Experience would help on a real Monday morning with four disruptions and no data. But what would actually save you is the team around you. If I am being pulled into operational firefighting, something has already gone wrong at a structural level. The goal is to have people in place who handle the resolution so that your decisions stay at the right altitude. 
That is a function of hiring, trust, and how you have built the operation over time. Whether that can be trained? Partly. But mostly it is built, not taught.
