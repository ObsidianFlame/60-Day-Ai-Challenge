Claude offers different models optimized for different types of work. Understanding when to use Haiku, Sonnet, Opus, and different reasoning effort levels helps you get better outputs while improving efficiency.

What I did:
We were tasked with creating a personalized Claude Strategy by choosing the right model before writing the 'perfect' prompt.
I studied the resources and then proceeded to build a Personalized Claude strategy based on my use case, using Claude Sonnet 4.6 (Thinking ON and Medium Effort) to produce the Strategy.

What I learned:

Selection balances 3 key considerations: Capability, Speed, Cost - Like in business.
Evaluate Capabilities, Speed, Cost and Effort (tuning effort is better than switching models)
Choosing the right Claude model: Haiku (quick, simple), Sonnet (efficient, versatile), Opus (Complex, high cost) = Different tools, different jobs
Sonnet is the daily driver - Strong reasoning, coding, writing, analysis, research and complex problem-solving. It is very versatile, majority of your work gets done here.
Opus is large reasoning specialist - Specialized complex tasks for advanced reasoning.

Choosing your model well helps to manage tokens, which are important when trying to get multi-step workflows done - 'Extended thinking' adapts the reasoning depth.
Haiku is the simplest, no complex reasoning. Sonnet for writing and content creation, multi-step problems and workflows and General purpose work. Opus is Deep research and analysis, complex reasoning that requires multiple steps, when accuracy is critical.
Always re-test assumptions with each new release, they are a separate training model.

Choosing the best model:
  1. Fast, cost-effective model: Test your use case, evaluate performance, upgrade only if necessary - Initial prototyping and development, Applications with tight latency requirements, Cost-sensitive implementation, High-volume, straightforward tasks.
  2. Most capable model: When intelligence and advanced capabilities are critical, start with most capable model and optimize for efficiency. Implement, Optimize prompts, evaluate, increase/decrease efficiency by moderating effort as you optimize workflows - Complex reasoning tasks, Scientific/mathematical, nuanced understanding, accuracy>cost, high-autonomy agentic work.
  3. Create benchmark tests specific to your use case, test your actual prompts and data, compare performance for accuracy, quality and handling ede cases, finally weigh performance and cost trade-offs.

  My personalised strategy showed me that my current work sits at the fringe of the recommended model for my use case, with the biggest practical habit shift being that I should utilise Projects, to update Project knowledge and cut prompting time in half, improve output quality and have a more efficient workflow.
  The rule of thumb was clear, if it's external: upgrade one tier, if internal/iterative: stay where you are.
